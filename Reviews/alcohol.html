Alcohol and Cancer

Alcohol is a ubiquitous in American, European, and East Asian cultures. Research of alcohol metabolism, however, suggests alcohol should be abstained for optimum health and to avoid diseases like cancers. Alcohol induces cancers[1],[2] by changing hormone concentrationsi,[3],[4],[5], producing toxic metabolites[6], and disrupting vitamin stores like folate[7].

Breast cancer is significantly linked with alcohol consumption[8]. Decades of epidemiological research reveal a 9-20%[9],[10],[11],[12],[13],[14] greater risk of breast cancer in women who drink one serving of alcohol per day and an additional 7-25%[15],[16],[17],[18],[19] of breast cancer risk for each additional drink per day. Post-menopausal[20] and folate-deficient[21] women are especially susceptible to alcohol-induced breast cancer. Beer exhibits the greatest correlation at low to moderate alcohol consumption[22] while liquor exhibits the greatest correlation at high alcohol consumption[23].

Pancreatic cancer is associated with alcohol consumption[24]. The consumption of 2-3 alcoholic servings per day is associated with a ~22% increase in the risk of pancreatic cancer[25]. The risk is proportional with alcohol consumption[26],[27], especially beer[28]. Alcohol consumption may induce chronic pancreatitis[29],[30] which evolves into pancreatic cancer.

Liver cancer is significantly associated with alcohol consumption[31]. The rates of liver cancer increase 17[32]-28[33]% in males and 17[34]-97[35]% in females from consuming 2 servings of alcohol from mixed sources per day, and a 190% increased risk[36] from consuming liquor specifically. Alcohol causes liver cancer by manipulating genes[37], lowing the concentration of endogenous cancer-protecting antioxidants[38], and through contributing to the development of cirrhosis and fatty liver.[39]

Colo-rectal cancer is associated with alcohol consumption[40],[41]. The risk of colo-rectal cancer increases 8-128%[42],[43],[44],[45],[46] . Beer consumption is more correlated with colo-rectal cancer than wine and liquor [47],[48],[49],[50], especially with rectal cancers[51]. Alcohol associated colo-rectal cancers are augmented by nutritional deficiencies in folate, methionine,[52] zinc[53], [54], and the dietary consumption of heme iron, which are a B vitamin, an amino acid, a mineral, and meat-sourced iron[55], respectively.

Oral and esophageal cancers are profoundly associated with alcohol consumption[56], [57]. The amounts of alcohol in mouthwashes are even associated with an increased risk of oral cancers[58]. Individuals who consume 2 servings of alcohol per day possess a 38[59]-75[60]% increased risk of oral, esophageal, and laryngeal cancers.

Alcohol is a notorious recreational drug. Alcohol consumption is suggested to cause breast, liver, pancreatic, colorectal, and oral/esophageal cancers by consuming only 3-7 alcoholic servings per week, or by even using alcohol-based mouthwash. Beer appears more carcinogenic than other alcoholic beverages of comparable alcohol content, perhaps as a consequence of "off-flavors" or additives. The cancer-conscious individual is best to avoid alcohol.
